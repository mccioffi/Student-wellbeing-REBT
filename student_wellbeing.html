1 From "I MUST" to "I'd Like To": Flexible Thinking
+

‚ùå The Problem: Rigid "Must" Thinking

"I must perform well. I absolutely must get good grades."

When you tell yourself you MUST succeed, you create unnecessary pressure. This rigid thinking generates anxiety, which actually makes it harder to perform well. You end up in a vicious cycle: pressure ‚Üí anxiety ‚Üí worse performance ‚Üí more pressure.

‚Üì

‚úì The Solution: Turn Demands into Strong Desires

"I would really like to perform well. I will do my best to get that grade, but I accept that it might not happen."

üí° Why This Works:

Flexible thinking removes unnecessary pressure and protects your mental health. It doesn't mean you care less‚Äîyou still care deeply‚Äîbut you stay realistic. This mindset actually helps you perform better because it reduces anxiety. Ask yourself: Is there a law of the universe that says this must happen? Usually not. There's a strong desire, and you can do your best, but that's as far as we can go.

2 Failure Doesn't Define You: Accepting Setbacks
+

‚ùå The Problem: Catastrophizing Failure

"If I fail, it's the end of the world and it means I am a failure."

When you see failure as catastrophic and as proof that you're a failure as a person, the fear becomes paralyzing. This fear generates intense anxiety that actually impacts your performance‚Äîtaking you further away from your goals. You might avoid challenges altogether or give up too easily.

‚Üì

‚úì The Solution: Accept Failure as Part of Life

"Failure is bad but not the end of the world, and it does not mean I am a failure."

üí° Why This Works:

Failure is inevitable because we are all fallible human beings. Accepting this as part of life removes the pressure and allows you to focus on doing well without fear. When you do experience setbacks, you can acknowledge they're difficult, but then get back up and work towards your next goal. You learn resilience.

üéØ Remember:

Just because you fail at something doesn't mean you ARE a failure.

You are a complex human being having a temporary setback, not a permanent failure.

3 You're More Than Your Grades: The Big 'I', Little 'i'

‚ùå The Problem: Self-Worth Based on Achievement

üìñ Theoretical Foundation

The principles and strategies presented in this guide are inspired by and taken from Rational Emotive Behaviour Therapy (REBT), developed by psychologist Albert Ellis. REBT is an evidence-based approach that recognizes the powerful connection between our thoughts, emotions, and behaviors. By changing unhelpful thinking patterns, we can improve our emotional wellbeing and achieve our goals more effectively.

REBT has been extensively researched and shown to be effective for managing stress, anxiety, and building resilience‚Äîparticularly relevant for student life!

Your thoughts can either support or hinder you. The good news is that you have the power to change unhelpful attitudes into helpful ones. Be kind to yourself as you grow and learn.
